ARRL, the national association for Amateur Radio™

The ARRL website is a rich source of information about all aspects of amateur radio.



License exam preparation

License exams are drawn from question pools that are published here.  Practice tests based on the question pools are offered at www.qrz.com/ht/ and  HamTestOnLine.  The No-Nonsense Study Guides by Dan Romanchik, KB6NU, are very good, and free.  More comprehensive study books are published by ARRL.



Learning Morse code

Morse code is no longer required to obtain an amateur license, but it is still a popular and highly reliable means of communication.  A good website for learning Morse Code is www.lcwo.net.  Weekly practice transmissions from W1AW, the ARRL station, are archived here.


The Curiosity Mars rover has an interesting application of Morse code—its wheel treads include square holes that imprint “. _ _ _    . _ _ .   . _ . .” (JPL) into the Martian surface as the wheels turn.  The marks are a visual odometer that help monitor how well Curiosity is traversing the surface.  Read about it here.



Listen to ham radio on the web

A software defined radio (SDR) performs most of its signal processing digitally.  Some ham operators have made their SDRs available on the web, so you can tune in and listen to ham conversations around the world with just your web browser.



What frequency bands are hot right now?

The website dxmaps.com maps recent amateur contacts, so you can see which bands are experiencing favorable propagation conditions.  Summer Sporadic-E openings on the 50 MHz (6 meter) band are unpredictable and always interesting.
